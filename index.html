Neural Networks

I have always loved the intersection between technology and life sciences, and that is why I am drawn to artifical neural networks. Artificial neural networks roughly simulate the way that biological neural networks (like your brain) propogate information. In my mind, the fact that artificial neural networks can perform heuristic tasks that your brain does (such as reading text) shows that they reveal some hidden, universal geometry of intelligence.

The last tensor in the neural network represent's its guess as to what the number that it is looking at is. Hopefully, the index of the output vector that is the brightest should correspond to the correct digit. During training, the backpropogation algorithm matematically optimizes the weights in the convolutional filters to light up the correct number in the output vector for every number that it sees, thus causing the neural network to "learn".

Bioinformatics

I am very interested in the idea of studying proteins in silico, or in other words on a computer. Inspired by programming I have done for the Bioinformatics lab at R.I.T. I have made a protein viewer that works in your browser.

When you learned about enzymes in biology class, you probably were shown boring blobs on a screen, and were not very interested. This enzyme is a type of Mitochondrial ATP-Synthase, which synthesizes ATP from a proton gradient in the mitochondrias in your cells.

tip: you get new colors every time you refresh

This is enzyme works like a tiny water wheel, forcing ATP molecules together, with the energy of protons flowing through it. It is fascinating to think of how this mechanism evolved on a tiny scale, and 1.5 billion years ago, and is strikingly similar to mechanisms that humans have invented.
